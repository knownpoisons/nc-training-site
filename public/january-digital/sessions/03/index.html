02  The framework

ICC — the one frame for everything.

Three letters for everything you ask AI, and every brief you take.

I
Instructions
The task. The job. The part everyone gets right.
C
Context
The big one — and the one everyone starves. Who you are, the brand's world, what's worked before. More context = dramatically better output.
C
Constraints
Tone, ratios, must-haves, what good looks like. Constraints aren't limiting — they get you to the right idea faster.

Constraints come from three places: the client (demand them), your own taste and knowledge, and Claude (who'll pull more out of you).

"It's not a trick, it's a framework. ICC, ICC, ICC. Once we lock this in, the rest of the program gets easier, using AI gets easier, outputs become infinitely better."— Jeremy Somers

★  Remember this

Talk, don't type.

Your fingers can't keep up with your brain. When you type, you're structuring sentences and thoughts and the overall shape all at once, and the thinking backs up behind the typing. Talking to Claude isn't transcription — it's the ultimate organizer of your brain. You talk as fast as you want, double back, change your mind mid-sentence — and it ignores the noise and keeps what matters.

The habit
For anything bigger than a one-liner, speak your ICC instead of typing it. AirPods in, phone in pocket, Claude voice mode on, and go for a walk — 10, 20, 60 minutes. The output is in a different league.
The interview block
Even after a good brief, there's more ICC in your head you haven't said. So make Claude pull it out — paste your context, then add the interview prompt below.
"Quick context on how we work: we structure everything as ICC — instructions, context, constraints. Interview me to fill those three out. Ask all the questions you need, short multiple choice where you can, a few at a time, and wait for my answers before moving on."

Claude doesn't know "ICC" out of the box — define it once and it adapts instantly. You stay in charge: when you've got enough, say "we're good, let's move on."

04  What to remember

Keep these close.

A vague brief kills the job — and the margin.
Rework, again and again, is where the money leaks out. The whole system exists to send work out once and get it back with minor tweaks.
Over-index on detail.
Better to collect information you don't use than to discover the gap when the client says "I hate this concept." Get everything you can up front.
Pretend you don't know the client.
When you ask for a brief, brief as if you've never worked the account. Lean on what you already know and you'll never be able to work a client you don't know.
Lead, don't serve.
Clients don't pay you to ask them what they want — they pay you for the leadership to get to the best outcome. Take the creative direction back.
Don't ask me — ask Claudette.
Half of getting good is having the conversation with Claude itself. Ask it how to set up Projects and global instructions for your own context — you walk away with the skill, not just the answer.
Async beats live.
Don't take creative feedback live in a room — information gets lost, the client fixates on the wrong thing. Record the briefing, feed it to Claude, deliver when it's done.
05  Homework

Do this before Session 4.

Read this — we didn't get to it live. We ran out of clock before the homework again, so here's the full version. One habit, one share.

1 · Run ICC by voice before every Claude session
Open the ICC page on the hub as your trigger — then turn on the mic and speak your instructions, context, and constraints. Don't type them.
For anything bigger than a quick question, go further: AirPods in, phone in pocket, Claude voice mode on, and walk while you brief it. Ten minutes minimum.
2 · Use the "interview me" block at least once
On one real task this week, paste your context and then add the interview prompt from above. Let Claude pull the ICC out of you.
Notice how much was in your head that you hadn't said. Stay in charge — when you've got enough, tell it "we're good, let's move on."
3 · Share one win in Slack
Find one prompt or one result that worked and drop it in #project-creative-training. Say what you asked and what came back.
The team that shares prompts levels up faster than the team that hoards them — when Christina cracks something, everyone gets to adapt it.
