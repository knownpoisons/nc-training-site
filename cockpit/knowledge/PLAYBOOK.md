# PLAYBOOK.md — Sales learnings for the draft engine
**Source: NotContent Sales Playbook — Greatest Hits (mined from 30+ calls, Jul 2026).**
**This file is injected into every outreach draft. It governs angle, voice, and what never gets written down.**

---

## The thesis (the line everything sells)
"Double the content in half the time. Which just sounds better. What we actually do is more like 10x the content in a tenth of the time, but it didn't have the same ring to it."

Sell speed, output, and money — not AI. Jem's own rule: avoid leading with the letters "AI." Clients are buying creative that's faster, cheaper per outcome, and indistinguishable from shot-in-studio. The word AI appears when the *economic buyer* is being sold the capability shift; it disappears in every other context.

## Voice — written outreach rules
- Fast, confident, self-interrupting rhythm. Triads ("faster, cheaper, more of it"). Short punchlines end paragraphs.
- Aussie casual confidence, but **no profanity in written outreach** — swearing is a live-call conviction tool, Jem adds it by hand if the relationship earns it.
- Signature vocabulary allowed in drafts: one to one, world building, pixel pushing, teach you to fish, the machine, let us cook, deliverable agnostic.
- The double-helix: self-deprecation and swagger in the same breath ("I taught myself all of this" + "and the work now hangs in retail unquestioned"). Use sparingly — once per email max.
- Honesty beats are the biggest trust generator: volunteer a limitation before they ask ("AI still can't blow a bubble-gum bubble — but it can do most things"). One per touch, maximum.
- Lifestyle-as-proof: the nomad line ("writing this from [wherever Jem is]") is evidence the async model works. One beat, never a story, only when true.

## Angle bank — match ONE proof story per touch, by audience
- **Agencies** → Herman Scheer: zero to full AI production, now selling AI services to their own clients. Kicker quote: "You taught a guy how to fish."
- **Brand / in-house teams** → Maesa: brand launch that used to take nine months → three months, $280k saved on that one launch — landed in Target while training was still running. Trained teams now run the AI model across 12+ brands; Maesa's own projection is savings in the tens of millions in the next year, attributed to Oshyia Savur, VP Marketing (**always their words, attributed — never a NotContent claim**). **This is the ONE canonical Maesa telling, drawn from the live training.notcontent.ai case study. The $380k and $300k variants are retired.**
- **Enterprise** → Cash App: full campaign shipped three weeks after training ended; production time cut to a tenth (roughly 90%), campaign output up around 30%. Plus the NDA-as-status beat: "most of our work you'll never see credited — which tells you who the clients are."
- **Skeptics (any audience)** → retail invisibility: work hanging in major US retailers and no one has ever asked if it's AI. Optional spice: the challenge bet — "pick the AI from the real and the campaign's free." Use the bet rarely; it's a closer, not an opener.
- **Competitive displacement** → the qualifying question: "Ask any AI trainer one thing: what have you shipped? Not demoed — shipped, into stores, past legal. Then ask us. Bring both answers to your CFO."

## Discovery-as-CTA (what the outreach asks for)
The three-buckets ask converts because it's answerable: "Tell me the three things in your workflow that take the most time, cost the most money, and annoy you the most — and I'll tell you on a 15-minute call which ones we can automate, and which we can't." The "which we can't" clause is load-bearing. Keep it.
The Scorecard is the zero-friction alternative CTA: two minutes, no pitch, it'll just tell you. One CTA per message, never both.

## Objection doctrine for drafts
**Never answer an objection nobody raised.** The Amazon/first-class pricing reframe, the variance disclosure, the process rules — these are RESPONSES for replies and calls, not content for cold touches. Pre-empting plants the objection.
When a reply raises them:
- "Shouldn't AI be cheaper?" → Amazon reframe: faster + more of it costs more everywhere else in life; instead we keep budget flat and multiply output.
- Quality fears → retail invisibility + "if it's not one to one, why would I bother?"
- "Our clients will reject AI" → the Photoshop taxonomy: "no one's ever asked if I used Photoshop. They don't care what tools; they care that it's good, fast, on brand."
- "We could do this ourselves" → welcome it, then the time question: "where is your team's time best spent?" (The "I'm so much better than you" version is Jem-only, live-only, never in writing.)

## Qualification — who gets pursued, who gets the newsletter
Ideal: real budget, weak or stretched internal creative resource, a nameable approver, willing to follow a protocol.
Walk-away flags (route to broadcast lane, don't sequence): tiny budget + strong internal creative team + committee approvals with no named signer. The St. James pattern. If they can't name who signs, they're not a prospect yet.

## Follow-up doctrine
- The follow-up IS the conversion. Post-call cadence: day 2 value touch (the Loom — "every vendor should send a video like you did"), day 7 a made-thing ("built this thinking about your ingredient story" — an asset, not a check-in), day 30 a news hook. Then park.
- Never a naked "just checking in." Every touch carries one new thing: a proof point, a made asset, a relevant shift in the field.
- Every call ends with the next step booked on the call — drafts that propose times beat drafts that ask for availability.

## Pricing posture in writing
- Never per-asset, never hourly. "We charge for the creative system."
- **Training starts at $50k.** That's the anchor in every draft. No per-head maths, no lower entry point except the half-day taster where the doc calls for it.
- Written quotes only, 30-day validity, and prices only move up: "our pricing always goes up — lock it while it's this."
- Money up front is policy, stated plainly, never apologised for.
- Soft scarcity is fine ("the quarter is filling; there's room for a few more things"). NEVER "we can always fit things in."

## HARD BANS — the draft engine must never write these
- Any unverifiable flex: "50,000 stores," "three of the four big tech giants," naming any unsigned or NDA client (Nike above all).
- Numbers not in PROOF.md, including retired/drifted variants of real ones ($380k, $300k, "nearly $4M").
- Charisma-gated lines: "I'm so much better than you," "bet my children," the crack-cocaine close, profanity.
- Framework stacking: one framework or ritual per message, maximum.
- Tool names and seat costs in outreach (Flora, Weavy, per-seat pricing) — that's the bill of materials; it invites the DIY calculation.
- More than one proof story per touch. Rotation, not accumulation.
- Anything that jokes about regulated categories, likeness, or replacing humans. Charming on a call; radioactive in writing.
